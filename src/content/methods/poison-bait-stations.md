## Quick Answer
Underground bait stations place zinc phosphide or diphacinone pellets directly in the tunnel where gophers feed. When done right, it's fast and effective. When done wrong, you poison a neighbor's dog or a hawk that eats the carcass. Read this whole page before you touch the bait.

## The Gear
- Registered underground gopher bait (zinc phosphide or diphacinone pellets)
- Soil probe
- Trowel or bait applicator tube
- Nitrile gloves
- Mask (for zinc phosphide — it off-gasses)
- Marker flags

## The Process
1. Probe around fresh mounds to locate the main lateral tunnel — you'll feel the probe drop 2–4 inches when it hits the void.
2. Open a small access hole with your trowel — just wide enough to insert the applicator.
3. Place the labeled dose of bait inside the tunnel, not at the entrance. Gophers are suspicious of openings.
4. Plug the hole completely with soil or a clod to block light. Light in the tunnel causes gophers to avoid and seal off the area.
5. Flag the location. Check after 3–5 days for new activity. Re-bait once if no take in 7 days, then switch methods.

## Bagger Pro-Tip
Zinc phosphide works fast but requires a mask and careful handling — it reacts with moisture and produces toxic gas. Diphacinone is slower (anticoagulant, takes 5–7 days) but is safer to handle and has lower secondary poisoning risk. If you have raptors or cats in the area, go diphacinone.

## Safety Warning
Keep all bait away from children and pets. Never place bait in open boxes or above ground. Secondary poisoning of raptors, dogs, and cats is a real risk with zinc phosphide — if wildlife is present, use traps instead. Check your local regulations; some bait products require a pest control license to purchase.
